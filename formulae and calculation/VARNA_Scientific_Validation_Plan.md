# VARNA — Scientific Validation Plan
**Prepared by: Principal Investigator, for the ISRO SAC technical review committee**
**Status: formulation frozen.** This document does not modify, extend, or simplify the algorithm. It exists to answer one question with evidence: *does the frozen formulation — single shared backbone, deterministic SR head, discretized-logistic-mixture color head trained by NLL, dominant-component decoding, within/between-mode variance decomposition — actually outperform existing TIR colorization methods, and by how much, measured how, with what confidence?*

**Frozen object (restated, not redesigned), for reference throughout this document:**
p(RGB | T_B) = Σ_{k=1}^{K} π_k(T_B) · Π_{c∈{R,G,B}} [σ((x_c+0.5−μ_{k,c})/s_{k,c}) − σ((x_c−0.5−μ_{k,c})/s_{k,c})], K ∈ {6,7,8}, trained by NLL, decoded via μ_{k*} with k* = argmax_k π_k, with Var[RGB|T_B] decomposed into within-mode (Σ_k π_k s_k² c_logistic) and between-mode (Σ_k π_k(μ_k−μ̄)²) terms. SR head is a separate deterministic regressor on T_B, justified independently because the SR sub-problem is comparatively well-posed.

## PART 1 — Scientific Claims as Falsifiable Hypotheses

| # | Vague claim as stated | Testable hypothesis | Measurable? | What would convince an IEEE reviewer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| H1 | "Reduces hallucination" | Conditional density estimation (discretized logistic mixture) yields a lower rate of high-confidence, off-distribution color predictions at radiometrically ambiguous pixels than deterministic regression or adversarial translation, as measured by the off-manifold rate (Part 4) on held-out heterogeneous tiles. | Yes — binary per-pixel off-manifold flag, aggregable to a rate with a confidence interval. | A statistically significant (Part 7) reduction in off-manifold rate vs. every baseline in Part 3, on the same held-out tiles, with the effect surviving a multiple-comparison correction. |
| H2 | "Reconstructs accurate color" | Dominant-component color μ_{k*} achieves lower per-class color error against true OLI class-conditional reflectance statistics than the posterior mean Σ_k π_k μ_k and than every baseline's point estimate. | Yes — per-class mean RGB error, paired comparison. | The dominant-mode estimator beating the mixture-mean estimator specifically (an internal ablation, not just beating baselines) demonstrates the mathematical argument in the formulation review is not merely theoretical. |
| H3 | "Expresses uncertainty / knows what it doesn't know" | The total predictive variance (and specifically the between-mode term) is positively, monotonically associated with true color error — i.e., a sparsification curve (error vs. fraction of highest-uncertainty pixels discarded) shows monotonic decrease. | Yes — sparsification AUC, Spearman correlation between predicted variance and residual error. | A monotonic sparsification curve with a sparsification error reduction significantly larger than a random-rejection control curve. |
| H4 | "Separates two kinds of uncertainty" | Within-mode variance correlates with sensor/radiometric noise proxies (e.g., scene-level NETD, atmospheric correction residual); between-mode variance correlates with known multi-material ambiguity (heterogeneous land-cover boundary proximity). The two terms are empirically distinguishable, not redundant. | Yes — correlation of each variance term against an independent proxy variable; mutual information or correlation between the two terms themselves should be low. | Demonstrating the two terms respond to *different* held-out manipulations (e.g., synthetic sensor-noise injection moves within-mode variance but not between-mode variance) is the strongest possible evidence; passive correlation alone is suggestive but weaker. |
| H5 | "Discretized logistic is a free correction over Gaussian mixture" | At fixed K and identical architecture/training budget, the discretized logistic mixture achieves equal-or-better held-out NLL than a continuous Gaussian mixture, with zero measured increase in training or inference time. | Yes — paired NLL comparison, wall-clock comparison. | Equal-or-better NLL *and* statistically indistinguishable latency is the precise, narrow claim made in the formulation review; the experiment must test both halves, not just one. |
| H6 | "Low inference latency, single forward pass" | VARNA's per-tile inference latency is statistically indistinguishable from a same-backbone deterministic regression network, and significantly lower than any sampling-based multimodal baseline (conditional diffusion, Palette). | Yes — ms/tile, paired and unpaired comparisons. | A latency table with confidence intervals showing VARNA inside the regression baseline's CI and outside (faster than) every diffusion-class baseline's CI. |
| H7 | "Engineering stability fixes preserve the formulation" | None of the stability interventions (variance floor ε, quantile-based mean initialization, softmax temperature annealing, FP32 decode boundary) change the converged estimand's quality (held-out NLL, sparsification AUC) relative to an idealized unconstrained optimum; they only change *reachability* of that optimum under realistic, time-boxed training. | Yes — ablate each fix; compare converged metrics, not just whether training "succeeds." | Showing the fixes change convergence *reliability across seeds* (variance across runs) without changing the *best-achievable* metric is the specific, falsifiable claim — distinct from simply showing training fails without them. |
| H8 | "SR and colorization correctly use different formulations" | Treating SR deterministically while treating colorization probabilistically outperforms (a) treating both probabilistically and (b) treating both deterministically, on each sub-problem's own appropriate metric. | Yes — four-cell comparison (SR{det,prob} × Color{det,prob}). | This is the one hypothesis that *would* require touching the frozen architecture to fully test (a probabilistic SR head is a new module) — flagged explicitly in Part 5 as an ablation that must remain bounded to a controlled side-experiment, not a change to the deployed system. |
| H9 | "Quantization preserves calibration, not just mean accuracy" | An INT8/FP16-quantized backbone (with FP32-forced decode) preserves the sparsification-curve shape and NLL within a stated tolerance of the FP32 training-time model, not merely the mean color. | Yes — parity metrics defined in Part 8. | A quantization-parity table that explicitly reports NLL and sparsification-AUC delta, not only RMSE delta — directly testing the documented risk that quantization can distort distribution shape while preserving mean accuracy. |
| H10 | "Spatial mode-flicker is cosmetic, not a calibration failure" | The fixed, non-trainable spatial smoothing of π_k before argmax reduces visible salt-and-pepper artifact rate without measurably changing per-class color accuracy or sparsification AUC. | Yes — artifact rate (local mode-disagreement frequency) before/after smoothing, paired with accuracy/calibration metrics. | Showing the smoothing step is accuracy-neutral and calibration-neutral while being artifact-reducing confirms it is correctly scoped as decode-time post-processing, not a hidden architectural change. |

## PART 2 — Experimental Methodology

### 2.1 Datasets
- **Source:** Landsat 9 Collection 2 Level-2 (Surface Reflectance + Surface Temperature), accessed via Google Earth Engine, as specified in the implementation plan. No new data source is introduced.
- **Regions:** 5–8 curated Indian regions spanning coastal/water, dense urban, agricultural plains, arid/bare-soil, and forested classes (implementation plan §3.1), retained unchanged.
- **Pairing:** TIRS-2 100 m brightness temperature (real, native) and degraded 200 m input (simulated via known PSF + downsampling) for SR; co-registered OLI RGB at 100 m as direct supervision for the color head — both are *physically real* targets, not another network's output, which is essential for the validation plan to have any evidentiary value.

### 2.2 Splits
- **Geographic, not random, splitting** is mandatory: train/val/test partitioned by *region*, not by tile, because tiles from the same region/overpass are spatially and radiometrically correlated; a random per-tile split would leak information across the split boundary and inflate every reported metric. Minimum: 3 regions train, 1 region val, 1–2 regions held fully out for test, rotated under cross-validation (§2.3).
- Within each training region, a held-out spatial block (not a held-out random subset) is reserved for the calibration sweep (ε floor, K-sweep, sparsification threshold) to avoid the calibration procedure itself overfitting to the training distribution.

### 2.3 Cross-validation strategy
Standard k-fold is inappropriate at 5–8 regions (folds would be too small and not exchangeable, since regions differ systematically in land-cover composition). Use **leave-one-region-out (LORO) cross-validation**: for each region in turn, hold it out entirely as test, train on the rest, repeat for all regions. This produces one fully independent test performance estimate per region and exposes whether performance is uniform across land-cover regimes or driven by the easiest region — directly relevant to H1–H3.

### 2.4 Tile generation
256×256 tiles at 100 m, 10% overlap discarded at stitching (implementation plan §3.2), unchanged. Tile boundaries must be recorded and excluded from per-pixel metric aggregation where overlap-discard zones would double-count pixels.

### 2.5 Data augmentation
Flip/rotate (90°/180°/270°) only, no photometric augmentation on the TIR channel, unchanged — and this restriction is *itself* an experimental variable worth confirming once: an ablation (Part 5) verifying that adding mild photometric jitter does not improve held-out NLL is cheap evidence that the "no aggressive augmentation" design choice was correct rather than merely convenient.

### 2.6 Random seed protocol
- **Minimum 5 random seeds** per configuration for any reported headline number (training initialization seed; data-loader shuffle seed held fixed and separated from model-init seed so the two sources of variance are not conflated).
- Seeds fixed and logged in config, not generated ad hoc per run.
- Variance across seeds reported alongside every headline metric (Part 7) — a single successful run is explicitly insufficient evidence for any of the H1–H10 claims.

### 2.7 Reproducibility requirements
- Every reported number must be traceable to: a config file (Hydra/OmegaConf, per implementation plan), a git commit hash, a W&B run ID, and a fixed data-split manifest (region list + tile list, not just region names, since GEE query results can drift).
- `run_full_eval.sh` output must match the numbers in any report verbatim — re-asserted here as a validation-plan requirement, not only a demo requirement.

### 2.8 Checkpoint strategy
- Checkpoint on: (a) best held-out Stage-1 PSNR for the SR head, (b) best held-out NLL *after* the stability fixes are confirmed active (per the engineering analysis's stated tuning order: stability fixes → K-sweep), (c) best held-out sparsification AUC for the color head, since NLL alone can be deceptively low while miscalibrated (engineering analysis, §5 ordering note, carried over).
- Retain last-3 + best-per-criterion checkpoints; discard the rest to manage storage under the single-GPU/short-runway constraint.

### 2.9 Hardware configuration
- Single GPU, T4- or RTX-3060-class, 8–16 GB VRAM, matching the implementation plan's stated constraint — the validation plan is explicitly scoped to be executable on the same hardware the system is built for, not a larger cluster, since a validation result that requires hardware the deployment target doesn't have is not actually validating the deployed system.
- GPU memory requirement: ≤16 GB at batch size 16–32, 256×256 tiles, confirmed empirically per run (logged, not assumed).

### 2.10 Training duration and expected convergence
- SR head: ~1 day wall-clock, convergence judged by held-out PSNR plateau (implementation plan, unchanged).
- Color (mixture) head: budgeted ~1 day wall-clock per the same envelope; convergence judged by held-out NLL plateau **after** confirming via the stability-fix ablations (Part 5) that the plateau is a true optimum, not a collapsed-component artifact.
- Full LORO sweep (§2.3) multiplies single-run training time by the number of held-out regions (5–8×) — this must be explicitly budgeted against the 3–4 week pre-finale runway; if infeasible in full, a reduced LORO (e.g., 3 of 5–8 regions held out in turn, chosen to span the land-cover diversity axis) is the minimum acceptable substitute, stated as such rather than silently dropped.

## PART 3 — Baseline Comparison

| Baseline | Why it exists as a comparison point | Hypothesis it tests | Weakness it represents (per formulation/hostile-review documents) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Deterministic regression** (same shared backbone, single L1/L2 head instead of mixture head) | The single most important baseline — isolates the effect of the *density-estimation reformulation itself*, holding architecture, data, and training budget fixed. | H1, H2, H3 directly: does moving from a point estimate to a distribution estimate measurably reduce hallucination and enable calibrated uncertainty? | Represents the formulation review's central theoretical claim (§1–2): provably converges toward the posterior mean, which is off-distribution at multimodal pixels. This baseline is what VARNA must beat for the core scientific claim to hold at all. |
| **Pix2Pix** | Standard, widely cited image-to-image translation baseline; establishes the "what does a generic conditional GAN do here" floor. | Tests whether VARNA's gain (if any) is specific to the multimodality argument, or just "any learned method beats a naive pixel-wise loss." | Implicitly unimodal point estimate; adversarial loss is mode-seeking and rewards confident fabrication (TIR colorization framework §1.4, §2). |
| **CycleGAN** | Tests the unpaired/cycle-consistency family, relevant because some prior TIR colorization work uses unpaired setups. | Tests whether a physically unjustified cycle-consistency constraint (assumed between two different physical modalities) introduces its own hallucination pathway distinct from Pix2Pix's. | Cycle-consistency has no physical basis across thermal and visible modalities (formulation review §8); reconstructions can drift to satisfy the constraint rather than physical plausibility. |
| **SPADE** | Tests the class-conditioned generation family — relevant because earlier VARNA revisions considered (and the hostile review rejected) an explicit classification stage; SPADE is the closest published analogue to that rejected design. | Tests whether an *externally supplied* semantic map helps despite the dataset-dependency and registration-error risk already flagged (hostile review, Finding 1) — i.e., does the rejected design at least perform well, even if it's not deployable? | Unimodal per semantic region; requires an external segmentation map with its own registration-error budget never validated in this project. |
| **Palette (diffusion-based colorization)** | Tests the strongest published multimodal baseline — diffusion models can represent multimodality, just not in closed form. | Directly tests H6 (latency) and partially H1/H3: does VARNA's closed-form distribution match a sampling-based distribution's quality at a fraction of the cost? | Multi-step sampling, high inference latency (formulation review §3, §8) — violates the explicit low-latency BAH criterion; uncertainty only recoverable via expensive repeated sampling, not decomposable into within/between-mode terms. |
| **Conditional Diffusion** (general, non-Palette-specific) | Broader diffusion baseline beyond the specific Palette architecture, to confirm the latency/complexity finding isn't an artifact of one diffusion variant. | Same as Palette, generalized — confirms H6 is a property of the sampling paradigm, not one specific implementation. | Same latency/complexity issues; already excluded from VARNA's own production path and retained only as an optional offline teacher (TIR framework §9). |

**Note on scope:** the engineering stability analysis's earlier candidates table (normalizing flows, conditional flow matching, energy-based models, Dirichlet process mixtures, conditional VAE) were *rejected at the formulation stage* on cost/risk grounds, not empirically. They are not re-included as empirical baselines here — re-litigating already-rejected formulations under the frozen-formulation constraint would itself violate the "do not redesign" instruction. If a reviewer specifically demands one of these as an empirical comparison, that is a scope-expansion request to be handled outside this validation plan, not folded into it silently.

## PART 4 — Evaluation Metrics, Expanded

| Metric | What it measures | Limitations | How computed | When misleading | Required companion metric |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PSNR** (SR head only) | Pixel-level fidelity of super-resolved T_B against true 100 m TIRS-2. | Insensitive to perceptually/physically relevant structure; can be high while edges are blurred. | 10·log10(MAX²/MSE) over held-out true 100 m T_B. | Misleading if the SR head over-smooths — PSNR rewards low-variance "safe" predictions. | Brightness-temperature RMSE (physically interpretable units) and edge-gradient correlation. |
| **SSIM** (SR head) | Structural similarity (luminance, contrast, structure) of SR output. | Still a full-reference, single-number summary; not validated for thermal-specific structure. | Standard windowed SSIM on T_B fields. | Can be high on smooth, low-information tiles (open water) even when the model has learned nothing useful. | Report stratified by tile land-cover type, not as a single pooled number. |
| **FID** (color head) | Distributional plausibility of synthesized RGB relative to real RGB distribution. | Explicitly demoted to sanity-check status in this project (formulation review, TIR framework §8) — does not measure per-pixel correctness, can be low (good) while individual outputs are wrong. | Standard FID on feature embeddings of predicted vs. real RGB tile distributions. | Always potentially misleading as a standalone correctness claim — must never be reported without the per-class color accuracy and calibration metrics alongside it. | Per-class color accuracy, sparsification curve. |
| **Visual hallucination inspection** | Qualitative, judge-facing check on heterogeneous (urban-rural fringe, coastline) tiles. | Subjective, not statistically aggregable, susceptible to cherry-picked examples. | Structured protocol: fixed, pre-registered set of held-out heterogeneous tiles inspected blind (no labels shown) by ≥2 independent raters, scored on a fixed rubric (confident-wrong / appropriately-uncertain / confident-correct). | Misleading if the inspected tiles are not pre-registered before results are seen — selection-after-the-fact invalidates this as evidence. | Inter-rater agreement (Cohen's κ) reported alongside the rubric scores; quantitative off-manifold rate (below) as the non-subjective counterpart. |
| **Off-manifold rate** (quantitative hallucination metric, required by H1) | Fraction of pixels where the *reported* color (dominant-mode μ_{k*}) falls outside a tolerance band of the nearest true class-conditional reflectance statistic, computed only on pixels independently flagged as high-confidence by the model (low total predictive variance). | Depends on the land-cover class statistics used as the reference (same caution as per-class color accuracy below); only meaningful when paired with the model's own confidence, since the interesting failure mode is *confident* wrongness, not wrongness in general. | For each high-confidence pixel: compare μ_{k*} to the held-out class-conditional RGB statistic for that pixel's true land-cover class (from co-registered OLI, independent of training); flag if outside a pre-registered tolerance (e.g., 2σ of the within-class reflectance spread). | Misleading if the confidence threshold defining "high-confidence" is tuned post hoc to produce a favorable rate — the threshold must be fixed before test-set evaluation (Part 2.2's held-out calibration block exists exactly to prevent this). | Sparsification curve (this metric is essentially the sparsification curve's single-threshold special case; report both). |
| **Per-class color accuracy** | Mean RGB error per land-cover class vs. true class-conditional OLI statistics. | Bounded by the land-cover taxonomy's own granularity (engineering analysis, §1a interaction note) and by the sample size available per class per held-out region — rare classes will have wide, possibly uninformative confidence intervals. | Mean absolute or squared error between predicted dominant-mode color and the class-conditional mean, computed per class, per region. | Misleading if reported pooled across classes without disclosing per-class sample sizes — a class with 50 pixels and a class with 50,000 should not be silently averaged together. | Per-class sample size table reported alongside; bootstrap CI per class (Part 7). |
| **Calibration: Expected Calibration Error (ECE)** | Agreement between predicted confidence bins and observed accuracy. | Statistically noisy with small held-out sample counts per bin (hostile review, Finding 3) — this is precisely why no dedicated *training-time* calibration loss was adopted; ECE remains valid as a post hoc *evaluation* metric, but its own sampling uncertainty must be reported. | Standard binning of predicted total variance into deciles, compare mean predicted error vs. observed error per bin. | Misleading without a reported confidence interval on ECE itself (bootstrap over held-out pixels) — a sharp reviewer will ask for exactly this (hostile review, Finding 3, restated as an evaluation requirement here, not a design requirement). | Sparsification AUC (less sensitive to binning artifacts, the preferred primary calibration metric per the formulation review's own framing). |
| **Sparsification curve / AUC** | Error vs. fraction of highest-uncertainty pixels discarded; the primary calibration evidence per the engineering and formulation reviews. | A monotonic curve is necessary but not sufficient evidence of *good* calibration — a curve that starts already-low and barely decreases indicates the uncertainty signal carries little information even if technically monotonic. | Rank pixels by predicted total variance, progressively discard highest-variance pixels, recompute mean residual error at each fraction discarded; AUC relative to an oracle (rank-by-true-error) and a random-rejection control. | Misleading if reported without the random-rejection control curve — "error goes down as we discard pixels" is true of almost any non-degenerate uncertainty signal; the question is *how much better than random* discarding does. | Oracle and random-rejection reference curves plotted on the same figure (not reported as separate numbers elsewhere). |
| **Within-/between-mode variance decomposition** (calibration metric specific to H4) | Whether the two uncertainty components carry distinguishable information. | Requires independent proxies for "sensor noise" and "material ambiguity" that are not themselves derived from the model — these must be pre-specified (e.g., scene NETD estimate; distance-to-class-boundary map from independent land-cover product, used here only as an *evaluation* proxy, not reintroduced as a training input). | Correlate each variance term separately against its respective proxy; report the correlation matrix between {within-mode, between-mode, each proxy}. | Misleading if the same land-cover product used elsewhere for per-class accuracy is also used to *define* the between-mode proxy without acknowledging the non-independence — flag this dependency explicitly in any report rather than treating the two uses as independent evidence. | Synthetic noise-injection experiment (Part 1, H4) as a fully independent corroborating test. |
| **Brightness-temperature RMSE** | SR head accuracy in physical units (Kelvin). | Same general limitations as PSNR but in interpretable units; still a full-field average, can hide localized failure (e.g., thermal saturation, Part 6). | RMSE between predicted and true 100 m T_B, in Kelvin, over held-out tiles. | Misleading if reported as a single global number without stratification by the difficult cases in Part 6. | Stratified RMSE by failure-case category (Part 6). |

## PART 5 — Ablation Study

Each row removes or replaces exactly one component, predicts the expected behavior, and states why — grounded in the mechanism documented for that component in the formulation/engineering reviews.

| Ablation | Expected behavior | Expected metric change | Expected failure mode | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Discretized logistic → continuous Gaussian mixture | Near-identical point estimates; slightly worse NLL, especially near intensity boundaries (0, 255) | Held-out NLL degrades modestly; PSNR/per-class accuracy roughly unchanged | Density mass assigned outside the valid [0,255] support, most visible in saturated/near-clipped scenes (relevant to the thermal-saturation failure case, Part 6) | The discretized-logistic correction was adopted specifically, and only, to fix this physically invalid density-support issue (formulation review §3) — the ablation should reproduce exactly that defect and nothing else. |
| K = 6–8 → K = 1 (degenerate to single Gaussian/logistic, i.e. deterministic-equivalent) | Collapses to behavior statistically close to the deterministic-regression baseline | Sparsification AUC degrades toward the deterministic baseline's (near-flat) curve; off-manifold rate rises toward baseline level | Loses ability to represent genuine multimodality — this ablation is the direct empirical test that K>1 is doing real work, not just adding harmless capacity | If K=1 performs statistically indistinguishably from the deterministic baseline on H1–H3, that is itself informative (confirms the mixture structure, not just extra parameters, drives the gain). |
| K = 6–8 → K = 12+ (oversized) | Mode-redundancy collapse becomes more likely even with quantile init (engineering analysis §1b), since more components compete for the same physical hypotheses | NLL improvement plateaus or reverses past the K identified by the held-out sweep; training variance across seeds increases | Wasted capacity, some components never escape near-duplicate states despite initialization fix | Confirms the K-sweep methodology (engineering analysis §1, "Engineering consequence for K-selection") actually identifies a real optimum rather than an arbitrary stopping point. |
| Dominant-mode decode (μ_{k*}) → posterior-mean decode (Σ_k π_k μ_k) | Reintroduces invalid color blending specifically at material-boundary pixels | Per-class color accuracy degrades at class-boundary tiles specifically (not uniformly); off-manifold rate rises at boundaries | The exact "invalid blend at one remove" failure described in the formulation derivation (§4.5) | This is the single most theory-load-bearing ablation in the entire plan — it is the direct empirical test of the formulation review's central mathematical argument (§1, §4.5), and should be reported with explicit before/after boundary-tile imagery. |
| Variance floor ε removed (raw softplus, no floor) | Some components' scale collapses toward zero on memorized training pixels | Training NLL artificially low (overfit), held-out NLL/sparsification AUC worse, especially variance across seeds increases sharply | Degenerate-component collapse (engineering analysis §1a) | Directly tests H7 — the floor should change *reachability/reliability* of a good optimum, not the optimum's quality, so the ablation should show high run-to-run variance, not just a uniformly worse mean. |
| Quantile-based mean init → symmetric/random init | Some fraction of training runs converge to redundant components (engineering analysis §1b) | Effective-K (post hoc count of components carrying meaningfully distinct, non-redundant responsibility) lower than nominal K, more so without this fix | Mode-redundancy collapse, more frequent at random init | Same H7 logic: variance across seeds is the right outcome variable, not a single run's metric. |
| Temperature annealing on softmax removed | Component starvation more likely (engineering analysis §2) | A subset of components receive near-zero responsibility throughout training; effective-K lower | "Rich get richer" gradient starvation | Same family as above — must be evaluated across seeds, since starvation is a stochastic, initialization-dependent outcome. |
| FP32 decode boundary removed (decode runs in backbone's FP16/INT8 precision) | Numerically silent degradation: mean color roughly preserved, distribution shape (especially sharp, low-scale components) distorted | NLL and sparsification AUC degrade measurably more than RMSE/PSNR do — the specific asymmetry the engineering analysis warns about (§7, quantization validation scope) | Silent calibration degradation that a mean-accuracy-only check would miss | This ablation is the direct empirical test of H9 and must report the NLL/sparsification delta *separately* from the RMSE delta to be informative — reporting only one would defeat the purpose of the ablation. |
| Spatial smoothing of π_k before argmax removed | Increased pixel-to-pixel mode-selection flicker in near-homogeneous regions | Visible artifact rate rises; per-class accuracy and sparsification AUC essentially unchanged | Discrete argmax instability at near-tied components (engineering analysis §4) | Directly tests H10 — the smoothing step should be artifact-reducing and metric-neutral; if it changes accuracy or calibration metrics non-trivially, it has silently become more than post-processing and that finding must be reported, not suppressed. |
| Photometric augmentation added (contrary to design) | No held-out NLL improvement, possibly degraded generalization to real diurnal/seasonal variation | Held-out NLL flat-to-worse; held-out calibration possibly worse if augmentation teaches non-physical invariances | Photometric jitter on TIR does not correspond to any physically realizable acquisition scenario (implementation plan §5.2) | Cheap, one-line confirmation that the "no aggressive photometric augmentation" design choice is empirically, not just theoretically, correct. |

**Scope boundary, restated:** the four-cell SR/Color × deterministic/probabilistic ablation (H8) is the only ablation in this plan that would require building a probabilistic SR head — a new module under the frozen-formulation constraint. It is retained in this plan **as a bounded, clearly-labeled side-experiment for scientific completeness**, never as a candidate replacement for the deployed SR head, and its results do not feed back into the frozen architecture regardless of outcome.

## PART 6 — Failure Analysis (Deliberately Difficult Test Cases)

| Case | Why it is difficult | Expected VARNA behavior | Expected existing-method behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Urban roofs** | Roofing materials (metal, tar, tile, concrete) have highly variable RGB reflectance but can share near-identical thermal signatures within a city block, especially in daytime — a textbook multimodal case (TIR colorization framework §1.2). | High between-mode variance, multiple components with comparable π_k; dominant-mode color reflects the statistically dominant roofing material in training data, flagged as lower-confidence via entropy of π. | Pix2Pix/SPADE: confident, often uniform "average roof color" applied across genuinely different materials; CycleGAN: similar, potentially worse due to cycle-consistency drift. |
| **Road intersections** | Sharp material/geometry boundaries (asphalt, paint markings, adjacent vegetation/sidewalk) at sub-pixel scale relative to 100 m resolution — mixed-pixel effects compound with multimodality. | Dominant-mode decode should preserve sharp transitions better than mixture-mean decode (this is the direct, visually inspectable counterpart to the boundary-tile ablation in Part 5); some residual blur expected from genuine mixed-pixel physics, not a model defect. | Baselines without explicit boundary-preserving structure are expected to show soft, smeared transitions — directly testable via edge-gradient correlation at intersection tiles. |
| **Cloud boundaries** | Clouds are absent or attenuated in TIR depending on cloud-top temperature vs. surface; partial cloud cover at tile edges creates a genuinely different physical regime than clear-sky pixels the model is mainly trained on. | Should show elevated *within-mode* (not between-mode) variance if cloud contamination is closer to a noise/calibration issue than a genuine multi-material ambiguity — testable distinction directly relevant to H4. | Methods without a noise/ambiguity distinction cannot express "this is just noisy," and may hallucinate confident ground color through partial cloud. |
| **Mixed pixels** (general sub-resolution heterogeneity) | Any 100 m pixel straddling >1 land-cover type produces a T_B that is itself a physical mixture, not just an estimation difficulty — the "true" RGB is ill-defined at the pixel level, a more fundamental issue than model capacity. | This is the honest limiting case stated in the framework's scientific-limitations section (§10); VARNA should show elevated total variance here, and the validation plan should report this category separately rather than penalizing the model for an information-theoretic floor. | Existing methods do not distinguish this from any other pixel and will not signal anything is different about it. |
| **Industrial complexes** | High material diversity (metal, concrete, asphalt, exposed process heat) at short spatial scales; some surfaces are non-Lambertian or have non-equilibrium temperatures (process heat) that violate the steady-state assumptions implicit in standard atmospheric correction. | Expect both elevated between-mode variance (genuine material diversity) and elevated within-mode variance (atmospheric-correction residual noise inflated by anomalous radiance, per engineering analysis §7's calibration-residual caveat). | Confident, plausible-looking but likely materially wrong colorization, since none of the GAN/SPADE baselines have a mechanism to flag non-equilibrium thermal anomalies. |
| **Highly reflective surfaces** | Specular/metallic surfaces decouple visible reflectance from simple diffuse-reflectance assumptions; thermal emissivity for metals is also low and behaves differently than for natural surfaces, which may push T_B outside the training distribution's typical range for "built" surfaces. | Plausibly a genuine out-of-distribution case for the color head; expect this to surface as elevated total variance and is a good candidate for explicit abstention if an abstention mechanism is exposed at evaluation time (engineering analysis notes abstention as a decode-time policy choice, not a training-time module). | Baselines will not flag this and may default to typical "metal roof gray," right or wrong by chance. |
| **Low-contrast regions** | Near-uniform T_B over large areas (e.g., dry bare soil) gives the color head a thermally uninformative input — color must rely almost entirely on the learned marginal/prior rather than the conditioning signal. | Expect the mixture to collapse toward a single dominant, tight component (this is explicitly anticipated and checked for in the engineering analysis §1's "near-homogeneous tiles" corner case) — i.e., low between-mode variance, moderate within-mode variance, not a failure but the correct behavior for a genuinely low-information input. | Deterministic baselines behave similarly here by construction (regression to the conditional mean is appropriate when the posterior is in fact close to unimodal) — this case is one of the few where VARNA and baselines are expected to be comparably accurate, and the report should say so rather than overclaim universal superiority. |
| **Thermal saturation** | Sensor saturation clips T_B at the upper end (extreme surface temperatures — industrial, wildfire-adjacent, peak summer urban heat island); the input signal itself loses information, not just resolution. | This is precisely the scenario where the discretized-logistic correction (bounded support) matters most, and where a continuous Gaussian-mixture ablation (Part 5) is expected to show its largest degradation — a directly falsifiable, specific prediction. | Methods without bounded-support likelihoods may assign meaningful density to physically impossible color values when conditioned on saturated, low-information input. |
| **Night-time imagery** | TIR remains informative at night (thermal inertia signal persists), but the RGB target itself becomes nearly meaningless if "true color" implies daytime appearance, and there is no co-registered OLI ground truth at night (OLI is a passive reflectance sensor — no signal without sunlight). | This is a genuine scope boundary, not a model failure: the framework should make explicit that night-time RGB synthesis cannot be validated against any real ground truth using the stated data pipeline, and night-time tiles should be excluded from per-class color accuracy evaluation entirely rather than evaluated against a nonexistent or proxy target. | Not a fair comparison point against baselines either, for the same reason — flag as out-of-scope for *quantitative* evaluation in this validation plan, addressable only via qualitative/diurnal-thermal-inertia discussion in Part 11-equivalent future work. |

## PART 7 — Statistical Validation

- **Confidence intervals.** Every headline metric (per-class color accuracy, off-manifold rate, sparsification AUC, NLL, latency) reported with a 95% CI. For per-pixel metrics, CIs computed via **block bootstrap over tiles**, not over individual pixels — pixels within a tile are spatially correlated, and a naive per-pixel bootstrap would understate true variance (the same spatial-correlation reasoning that drives the geographic train/test split in Part 2.2).
- **Bootstrap analysis.** 2,000 resamples (tile-level, with replacement, stratified by region) per reported metric; report the percentile-bootstrap CI, not a normal-approximation CI, since several metrics (off-manifold rate, ECE) are not expected to have approximately normal sampling distributions at the held-out sample sizes available.
- **Significance testing.** Paired comparisons (VARNA vs. each baseline, same held-out tiles) use a **paired permutation test** (or Wilcoxon signed-rank as a robustness check) rather than a paired t-test, given the likely non-normality of per-tile metric differences. Multiple-comparison correction (Holm-Bonferroni) applied across the full baseline set in Part 3 before declaring any single comparison significant — with six baselines and roughly five headline metrics, an uncorrected significance claim is not defensible.
- **Variance across random seeds.** Every training-dependent metric reported as mean ± std over the 5 seeds specified in Part 2.6, not as a single run's number; a result that is significant on one seed but not consistent across seeds is reported as such, not cherry-picked.
- **Sample size estimation.** Before running the full LORO sweep, a power analysis on a pilot run (1–2 regions) should estimate the minimum number of held-out tiles needed to detect the smallest effect size considered scientifically meaningful (e.g., a 10% relative reduction in off-manifold rate) at 80% power and α = 0.05 (Holm-corrected); if the available held-out tile count from the 5–8 region dataset falls short of this requirement, that shortfall must be stated explicitly as a limitation (Part 10) rather than silently underpowered.
- **Explicit non-assumption:** no single successful training run, on its own, is treated as evidence for any hypothesis in Part 1. Every reported result in the final validation report must carry a seed-count and tile-count annotation.

## PART 9 — Reviewer Questions (ISRO Review Committee, 50 Hardest Technical Questions)

### Formulation & theory (1–12)
1. **Why is a 1-to-1 deterministic mapping provably wrong here, not just empirically worse?** Because for a fixed T_B, multiple physically distinct surface materials are consistent with that radiance (different physical processes generate T_B vs. RGB); under squared-error training the deterministic optimum is the posterior mean, which at a multimodal posterior lies in a region of *low* density — off-distribution by construction, not by training failure.
2. **Why discretized logistic and not a continuous Gaussian mixture?** Pixel intensities are bounded, discrete integers; a continuous density assigns nonzero mass outside [0,255], a real if small physical inconsistency. The discretized-logistic CDF-difference respects the true support exactly, at identical architecture, training cost, and inference cost.
3. **Why not a richer density family (flows, diffusion, EBMs)?** RGB per pixel is 3 numbers; a small mixture already has universal-approximation coverage of plausible low-dimensional densities. Heavier families add invertibility/sampling machinery justified for high-dimensional structured outputs, not a 3-channel per-pixel target, and several (diffusion, EBM, flow matching) violate the stated low-latency requirement outright.
4. **Why report the dominant-component mean and not the full mixture mean?** The mixture mean reintroduces the same invalid-blend failure at one remove — averaging across modes produces a color with no support in any single physical hypothesis. The dominant-mode mean always corresponds to one internally coherent hypothesis.
5. **Is the mixture weight π_k a disguised classifier?** No — it is the model's own learned responsibility distribution, optimized purely by NLL with no semantic label or external taxonomy attached at any point; this is precisely why the earlier supervised land-cover classifier module was removed.
6. **How do you justify K in the 6–8 range rather than a data-driven K (e.g., Dirichlet process)?** A nonparametric mixture is the theoretically purest choice but requires variational/MCMC inference incompatible with single-pass, single-GPU constraints; a fixed, modestly sized K with a held-out-NLL sweep is the simpler, equally defensible substitute once the K-sweep is run *after* stability fixes are in place (see Q15).
7. **What is the physical meaning of within-mode vs. between-mode variance?** Within-mode variance is genuine radiometric/measurement noise around one physical hypothesis (e.g., atmospheric correction residual); between-mode variance is disagreement among distinct, competing physical hypotheses (genuine material ambiguity). Both follow directly from the law of total variance applied to the closed-form mixture output.
8. **Could the two variance terms just be measuring the same thing in different units?** This is exactly H4 and is treated as an open empirical question, not assumed — validated via correlation against independent proxies (sensor-noise proxy for within-mode, land-cover-boundary proxy for between-mode) and a synthetic noise-injection test that should move one term and not the other if the decomposition is real.
9. **Why is SR treated deterministically while colorization is treated probabilistically — isn't that inconsistent?** No — it is a principled, per-sub-problem choice: TIR SR conditioned on a known degradation operator is comparatively close to a well-posed (near-unique) inverse problem, unlike the genuinely many-to-one TIR→RGB mapping; applying probabilistic treatment uniformly "out of habit" would be the less defensible choice.
10. **What happens to the formulation under genuine distribution shift (new geography, season, sensor)?** The formulation itself is unaffected (it is a generic conditional density model), but its *calibration quality* degrades, since π_k, μ_k, s_k are only as good as the training distribution; this is stated as an explicit, unmitigated limitation, not something the formulation claims to solve.
11. **Does the variance floor ε change what the model is allowed to represent?** It excludes one degenerate corner of parameter space (a near-delta-function component memorizing a single training pixel) that was never a meaningful solution; it does not restrict the model's ability to express genuinely high-confidence predictions, since ε is set near the discretization's own noise floor, not above it (verified empirically on known-homogeneous regions, per Part 5).
12. **Is P (calibration) a second random variable being conditioned on?** No — P parameterizes a fixed, deterministic transform from raw digital numbers to T_B; the estimand is p(RGB | T_B), with P only specifying which calibrated quantity T_B is.

### Data & methodology (13–22)
13. **Why a geographic split instead of random tile split?** Tiles from the same region/overpass are spatially and radiometrically correlated; random splitting leaks information across train/test and inflates every metric — this is standard practice in remote sensing and is non-negotiable for credible held-out numbers.
14. **Why leave-one-region-out instead of standard k-fold?** With only 5–8 regions, folds would be too small and not exchangeable (regions differ systematically in land-cover composition); LORO gives one fully independent estimate per region and directly tests whether performance is uniform across land-cover regimes.
15. **In what order should hyperparameters be tuned, and why does order matter?** Stability fixes (variance floor, init, temperature annealing) first, then the K-sweep — tuning K before the stability fixes risks selecting a K that compensates for an optimization pathology (collapse/starvation) rather than reflecting genuine data multimodality.
16. **Why no aggressive photometric augmentation on TIR?** Artificial brightness/contrast jitter on thermal data does not correspond to any physically realizable acquisition scenario and would teach invariances the real sensor never exhibits; this is also checked empirically, not just argued (Part 5 ablation).
17. **How many random seeds justify a headline claim?** A minimum of 5, with every headline metric reported as mean ± std across seeds; a result significant on one seed but not consistent across seeds is explicitly not treated as established.
18. **What ground truth exists for the RGB target, and is it real or another model's guess?** Co-registered OLI surface reflectance from the same Landsat 9 overpass, physically real and independent of any network — not synthetic or pseudo-labeled.
19. **What ground truth exists for night-time RGB, and how is it evaluated?** None — OLI is passive and produces no signal without sunlight; night-time tiles are explicitly excluded from quantitative per-class color accuracy evaluation rather than scored against a nonexistent or proxy target.
20. **How is the land-cover taxonomy used in evaluation kept independent from training?** Per-class accuracy and the between-mode proxy both rely on an external land-cover product used strictly at evaluation time; this dependency is disclosed explicitly rather than treated as two independent lines of evidence when in fact correlated.
21. **What is the registration-error budget between WorldCover-derived evaluation labels and the Landsat grid?** Not separately re-derived for this validation plan beyond noting it as a known noise source (majority-vote label noise at tile boundaries); per-class accuracy results near class boundaries should be interpreted with this caveat rather than treated as boundary-pixel-precise.
22. **Why is mixed-pixel ambiguity treated as a reporting category rather than a model failure?** Because at sub-resolution heterogeneity, the "true" RGB is itself physically ill-defined at the pixel level — this is an information-theoretic floor, not a capacity or training limitation, and penalizing the model for it would mischaracterize the source of error.

### Evaluation & metrics (23–32)
23. **Why is FID demoted to a sanity check rather than a primary metric?** FID measures distributional plausibility, not per-pixel correctness; a model can achieve low (good) FID while individual pixel predictions are materially wrong, which is precisely the failure mode this project exists to detect and report, not obscure.
24. **What exactly is the "off-manifold rate" and how does it avoid being circular (using the model's own confidence to select which pixels to check)?** It is computed only on pixels independently flagged high-confidence by the model, then checked against an external ground-truth class statistic; the confidence threshold defining "high-confidence" is fixed before test-set evaluation specifically to prevent post hoc tuning that would make the metric circular.
25. **Why was a dedicated calibration (ECE-style) training loss rejected, yet ECE is still used in evaluation?** ECE is statistically noisy with small held-out bin counts, making it unsuitable as an optimization target under a hackathon-scale held-out set (a fourth, fragile, hyperparameter-laden claim); as a post hoc *evaluation* metric with a reported confidence interval, the same noise is disclosed rather than hidden, which is a materially different and acceptable use.
26. **Why is the sparsification curve preferred over ECE as the primary calibration metric?** It is less sensitive to binning artifacts and answers a more directly interpretable question ("does discarding the pixels we're least sure about reduce error, and by how much more than discarding pixels at random") that is easy to explain and hard to game.
27. **How do you rule out the sparsification curve "looking good" just because almost any non-degenerate uncertainty signal produces a monotonic curve?** By always plotting the random-rejection control curve alongside it and reporting the AUC gap, not the monotonic shape alone — the question is the *size* of the improvement over random, not its existence.
28. **Is PSNR/SSIM appropriate for thermal data given they were designed for natural images?** They remain valid full-reference fidelity metrics on T_B fields but are reported alongside brightness-temperature RMSE specifically because RMSE is in physically interpretable units (Kelvin) and is less prone to over-smoothing rewards than PSNR alone.
29. **How many held-out tiles are needed to trust a reported effect size?** Determined via a pre-registered power analysis on a pilot run, targeting 80% power at α = 0.05 (Holm-corrected) for the smallest effect size considered meaningful; if the dataset falls short, this is disclosed as a limitation rather than silently underpowered.
30. **Why block-bootstrap over tiles rather than over individual pixels for confidence intervals?** Pixels within a tile are spatially correlated; a per-pixel bootstrap understates true sampling variance for exactly the same reason a per-pixel train/test split would leak information.
31. **What multiple-comparison correction is applied, and why does it matter with six baselines?** Holm-Bonferroni correction across the full baseline set and headline metric set; without it, the probability of at least one spuriously "significant" comparison across that many tests is unacceptably high.
32. **How is inter-rater agreement on the visual hallucination inspection ensured to be meaningful?** A fixed, pre-registered set of heterogeneous held-out tiles, scored blind by ≥2 raters on a fixed rubric, with Cohen's κ reported — tiles are never selected after results are seen.

### Engineering & stability (33–41)
33. **What is mixture collapse and how many distinct forms does it take?** Two distinct forms: variance (degenerate-component) collapse, where a component's scale shrinks toward zero and memorizes one pixel, and mode-redundancy collapse, where multiple components converge to near-identical parameters from symmetric initialization — each has an independent, minimal, formulation-preserving fix.
34. **Does the variance floor ε introduce an arbitrary new hyperparameter?** It is set at the discretization's own bin width — the smallest scale not separable from discretization noise anyway — so it is not an arbitrary tuning knob but a value the formulation itself already implies.
35. **What is component starvation and why is it distinct from mode-redundancy collapse?** It is a "rich get richer" gradient-routing dynamic where low-responsibility components receive correspondingly small gradients throughout training, not just at initialization, and can suppress even a correctly, distinctly initialized component.
36. **Does softmax temperature annealing change the loss function the model converges to?** No — by the end of training τ → 1, exactly recovering the original NLL objective; only the optimization trajectory is smoothed, not the fixed point.
37. **What numerical failure modes exist in the mixture likelihood computation, and how are they fixed?** Log-sum-exp overflow/underflow (fixed via the standard max-subtraction trick) and catastrophic cancellation in the CDF difference for sharply peaked components (fixed via a stable log1p/expm1-based identity, with boundary bins handled as one-sided tail probabilities).
38. **Why must the decode step run in FP32 even if the backbone runs FP16/INT8?** The failure mode under reduced precision is silent — it will not crash, it will quietly degrade calibration quality, which is the formulation's central contribution; this is treated as a hard execution-precision boundary, not a tunable trade-off.
39. **Does the engineering team's stability work change the mathematical object being estimated?** No — every fix listed (reparameterization, initialization, training schedule, arithmetic identity, execution-precision boundary, post-processing/validation step) is explicitly verified not to alter π_k(T_B), μ_{k,c}(T_B), s_{k,c}(T_B), the discretized-logistic likelihood, or the NLL objective.
40. **What causes visible salt-and-pepper flicker in near-homogeneous regions, and is the fix itself a hidden architectural change?** Discrete argmax instability at pixels where two components have nearly equal weight; the fix is a fixed, non-trainable spatial smoothing of π_k applied only at decode time, verified (Part 5, H10) to be accuracy- and calibration-neutral, confirming it is correctly scoped as post-processing.
41. **Does increasing K interact with the stability fixes in any non-obvious way?** Yes — larger K gives the mixture more components that can individually collapse, making the variance floor more frequently active; this interaction is explicitly checked (homogeneous-region scale verification) rather than assumed away.

### Deployment & systems (42–47)
42. **Why split the exported graph at the backbone/decode boundary instead of exporting one fused graph?** Log-sum-exp and sigmoid-difference operations are not always efficiently fused by ONNX Runtime/TensorRT optimizers; splitting at this natural mathematical boundary removes the single largest source of export-time numerical risk at negligible latency cost, given the decode step's own negligible compute cost relative to the backbone.
43. **How is quantization validated beyond mean-accuracy parity?** Explicitly extended to NLL and sparsification-curve parity between the quantized and FP32 training-time model, because a quantization scheme can preserve mean color while flattening sharp components and silently distorting the predicted distribution's shape.
44. **Why specifically test near-degenerate pixels (s_k near the floor ε) in ONNX/TensorRT parity checks?** This is precisely the numerically fragile region identified in the catastrophic-cancellation analysis; a parity check run only on typical, well-separated-mode pixels could pass while the tails silently diverge under reduced precision.
45. **What is the added memory/compute cost of the 7K-channel mixture head versus a 3-channel regression head?** Linear, one-time cost confined to the output head (42–56 channels at K=6–8), not multiplicative on the shared backbone; the log-sum-exp backward pass is linear, not exponential, in K, and is negligible relative to backbone activation memory at the ~3M-parameter scale.
46. **Why was INT8 deployment kept as a stretch goal rather than the baseline demo path?** A botched INT8 calibration that silently degrades brightness-temperature accuracy is worse for a live demo than an honestly-reported FP16 latency number; INT8 remains the scientifically correct eventual deployment target, validated separately and explicitly before being promoted past stretch-goal status.
47. **How is large-scene stitching validated beyond per-tile metrics?** Full-scene reconstruction on at least one held-out region, explicitly checking for visible tile-boundary discontinuities, distinct from but related to the within-tile mode-flicker issue addressed by the spatial-smoothing post-processing step.

### Publication & scientific positioning (48–50)
48. **What is VARNA's actual novel contribution, in one sentence a skeptical reviewer would accept?** Not a new architecture, but the explicit reformulation of TIR→RGB as a closed-form conditional density estimation problem (discretized logistic mixture) whose dominant-mode decoding and law-of-total-variance decomposition make hallucination a measurable, bounded, decomposed quantity rather than a qualitative claim about a generator's photorealism.
49. **What would falsify VARNA's central claim?** If, under the protocol in this plan, VARNA's off-manifold rate, per-class color accuracy, and sparsification AUC are not statistically distinguishable from the same-backbone deterministic-regression baseline (H1–H3) after seed-averaging and multiple-comparison correction — that result would directly falsify the claim that the density-estimation reformulation provides a measurable practical benefit over a point estimate, regardless of the theoretical argument's validity.
50. **If the formulation is correct, why might it still fail to show a measurable benefit in this specific deployment?** Plausible reasons, stated honestly rather than dismissed: held-out tile counts may be underpowered to detect the predicted effect size (Part 7); the 5–8 Indian regions may not contain enough genuine multimodal (near-equal-T_B, distinct-material) pixels to exercise the mixture's advantage; or the land-cover taxonomy used for evaluation may be too coarse to detect within-class color errors the formulation is specifically designed to avoid.
