# VARNA — Formulation Review: Is Conditional Density Estimation the Correct Mathematical Object?

**Reviewing body:** Senior ISRO SAC Scientist, Remote Sensing Scientist, Thermal Imaging Physicist, Computer Vision Researcher, HPC Engineer, IEEE TGRS Reviewer, BAH 2026 Technical Evaluator.
**Mandate:** verify, not improve. Replace only if a strictly stronger and still hackathon-feasible alternative exists.

---

## 1. Is conditional density estimation the correct formulation?

**Yes, and the proof is a decision-theoretic one, not an aesthetic preference.**

Let T_B denote calibrated brightness temperature at a pixel and RGB the corresponding visible reflectance triplet. The physical relationship established in the original problem analysis is that T_B and RGB are generated by different physical processes (thermal emission vs. solar reflection) that share only an indirect common cause — surface material. For a fixed T_B, multiple materials are consistent with that radiance (§1.2 of the original proposal), so the true generative relationship is

RGB | T_B ~ a genuinely multimodal distribution, not a function.

Given this, ask what object a model is allowed to output. A deterministic model f_θ(T_B) commits, by construction, to a single point. Standard decision theory says: under squared-error training, the Bayes-optimal point a network converges to is the **conditional expectation** E[RGB | T_B] — the posterior mean. When the true posterior is multimodal (two well-separated materials with similar T_B), the posterior mean lies **between** the modes, in a region the posterior itself assigns *low* density. The "optimal" deterministic output is therefore, provably, not merely imprecise but **off-distribution** — a color that does not correspond to any plausible material at that pixel. This is not a training failure to be fixed with a better network; it is what minimizing expected squared error *means* whenever the target is multimodal.

A density estimate p(RGB | T_B) does not have this defect, because it is not required to collapse the distribution to a single number; collapsing to a reportable color becomes a downstream decision (taking the mode of the dominant component, not the mean of the whole distribution — this distinction is load-bearing and is made explicit in §4 below). The formulation is therefore correct because it is the only one of the two objects (point estimate vs. distribution estimate) that can, even in principle, represent the true generative process without systematic bias toward physically invalid outputs.

## 2. Why is deterministic regression mathematically insufficient?

Three independent arguments, each sufficient on its own.

**Information theory.** The mutual information I(T_B ; RGB) is bounded above by the information that survives the physical chain "surface material → (emissivity, temperature)" and "surface material → spectral reflectance" sharing only the material as a common cause, not a deterministic link. Formally, T_B and RGB are conditionally near-independent given material class, but material class is *not observed* — only T_B is. By the data-processing inequality, no function of T_B alone can recover more information about RGB than I(T_B ; RGB) permits, and this quantity is empirically and physically small relative to H(RGB | material), the entropy of color *within* a material class is itself non-trivial; across classes that share thermal signatures, the residual conditional entropy H(RGB | T_B) is strictly positive and, by the argument above, not small. A deterministic regressor implicitly assumes H(RGB | T_B) ≈ 0 — i.e., that T_B determines RGB. This assumption is false by construction of the underlying physics, and the model's output is not the data's actual conditional law; it is the closest deterministic function to it.

**Inverse problems.** TIR → RGB is ill-posed in the classical Hadamard sense, but the relevant violation here is **uniqueness**, not just stability: for a given observed T_B, there is no unique RGB that the forward physical model implies, because the forward maps (emission and reflection) are governed by independent surface parameters. Standard inverse-problem regularization (Tikhonov, total variation, etc.) is the correct tool when the forward map is many-to-one only due to noise or rank deficiency around an otherwise well-defined unique solution — i.e., when stability, not uniqueness, is the failure mode. Here, multiple *genuinely distinct, equally physically valid* solutions exist. The mathematically appropriate generalization of regularized inversion to a genuinely non-unique inverse problem is to characterize the **set** (or distribution) of consistent solutions, not to regularize toward one. This is, again, exactly conditional density estimation.

**Bayesian inference / statistical learning theory.** In a Bayesian treatment, p(RGB | T_B) is the actual posterior; a deterministic estimator is a specific summary statistic of that posterior (its mean, under squared loss; its mode, under 0/1 loss; etc.). The choice of summary statistic discards information — specifically, it discards the posterior's *shape*, which is exactly the information that distinguishes "this pixel is unambiguous" from "this pixel could plausibly be one of two very different colors." Under standard statistical learning theory, a model trained to output only the conditional mean is asymptotically consistent for that mean, but the mean is not a sufficient statistic for the full conditional law unless the posterior is unimodal and symmetric — an assumption with no physical justification here, and the original proposal already explicitly rejected this assumption in its first-principles analysis (§1.2). A model architecture must be expressive enough to represent the actual estimand; a single-output regressor is not, and no amount of additional training data or capacity changes that, because the limitation is in the *output representation*, not the optimization.

## 3. Is a Gaussian mixture the right representation, or is something stronger available?

The original formulation specified a Gaussian Mixture Network (or "superior alternative if one exists"). This review identifies one defensible strengthening and rejects five heavier alternatives, on the same grounds the team should expect a panel to apply.

| Candidate | Mathematical correctness | Computational complexity | Training stability | Inference latency | 4-engineer / BAH feasibility | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gaussian Mixture (K components)** | Correct in spirit; assigns nonzero density outside the valid [0,255] (or [0,1]) support of pixel intensities — a known, minor physical inconsistency | Low | High (closed-form NLL) | Single forward pass | High | Baseline — improvable at near-zero cost (see below) |
| **Discretized logistic mixture** (PixelCNN++-style) | Same mixture structure, but likelihood is a CDF difference that respects the bounded, discrete support of real pixel values exactly | Identical to GMM — same network output shape, different final likelihood formula | Identical | Identical (still one forward pass) | Identical engineering effort to GMM | **Adopted.** Same head, same training loop, corrects a real (if small) physical inconsistency for free |
| Normalizing flows (conditional) | Correct, exact likelihood | Moderate–high — requires invertible layer constraints (coupling layers, tractable Jacobians) | Moderate; sensitive to layer design | Single pass, but heavier per-pass cost than a mixture head | Disproportionate engineering cost for a **3-dimensional** output | Rejected — Occam's razor: invertibility machinery is built for high-dimensional structured outputs; RGB per pixel is 3 numbers, where a small mixture already has universal-approximation coverage of plausible densities |
| Conditional flow matching | Correct, flexible | High — requires solving an ODE (multiple integration steps) at inference | Requires careful vector-field training | Multi-step sampling → violates low-latency requirement | Beyond a 4-engineer, multi-week budget to validate properly | Rejected on latency grounds alone |
| Energy-based models | Correct, very flexible (unnormalized) | High — no closed-form normalizer; training needs contrastive divergence or score matching | Known to be unstable without extensive tuning | Sampling requires iterative MCMC/Langevin steps | High risk of non-convergence under deadline | Rejected — same risk class as joint end-to-end training without a stabilizing structure, already rejected in the implementation-feasibility review |
| Dirichlet process mixture | Theoretically the "purest" generalization — nonparametric, infers K automatically | High — requires variational or MCMC inference over the number of components | Implementation-heavy, slow to converge | Not single-pass in any practical hackathon implementation | Disproportionate to the actual gain, since a small fixed K already captures the empirically observed number of plausible materials per local context | Rejected — fixed, modestly sized K with a held-out-NLL sweep is the simpler, equally defensible substitute |
| Conditional VAE | Correct in principle | Adds an entirely separate encoder network used only at training time; output distribution becomes implicit (sampling-based), not closed-form | Requires KL-term balancing | Confidence computation needs Monte Carlo sampling unless restricted to a single Gaussian decoder — which then has no more expressive power than the mixture head already proposed | Adds indirection with no expressiveness gain at this output dimensionality | Rejected |
| **Conditional diffusion** | Correct, currently most expressive in image-translation literature | High | Moderate, with enough data | Multi-step denoising — directly violates the explicit "low inference time per tile" BAH evaluation criterion | Already rejected from the production path in the implementation review; retained only as an optional offline teacher | Rejected for deployment, unchanged from prior review |

**Conclusion: the strengthening is real but small.** Replace the continuous Gaussian mixture with a **discretized logistic mixture** — same architecture, same training loop, same single forward pass, only the final likelihood formula changes from a Gaussian density to a discretized-logistic CDF difference. This is the only modification in this entire review that improves the formulation's physical correctness while costing the team approximately zero extra implementation time, which is exactly the bar this exercise sets.

## 4. Complete mathematical derivation

**4.1 — Role of P.** P denotes the deterministic physical preprocessing (sensor calibration constants, inverse Planck transform) applied before any learned component sees the data. P is not a second random variable being conditioned on; it parameterizes which fixed, known transform g(·; P) converts raw digital numbers to T_B. Formally:

T_B = g(DN_raw; P), and the estimand is p(RGB | T_B), with P entering only through the deterministic construction of T_B itself. This is why P appears in the original notation P(RGB | T_B, P) without being a separate stochastic input — it specifies *which calibrated quantity* T_B is.

**4.2 — Parametric form (discretized logistic mixture).**

p(RGB | T_B) = Σ_{k=1}^{K} π_k(T_B) · Π_{c ∈ {R,G,B}} [ σ((x_c + 0.5 − μ_{k,c}(T_B)) / s_{k,c}(T_B)) − σ((x_c − 0.5 − μ_{k,c}(T_B)) / s_{k,c}(T_B)) ]

where σ is the logistic sigmoid, x_c is the integer pixel intensity in channel c, μ_{k,c} and s_{k,c} are the location and scale of mixture component k for channel c, and π_k(T_B) are mixture weights (Σ_k π_k = 1, π_k ≥ 0) produced by a softmax. Each factor is the probability mass the discretized logistic places on the integer bin [x_c − 0.5, x_c + 0.5], which by construction cannot assign density to physically invalid intensities outside [0, 255] — the correction motivated in §3.

**4.3 — Network.** A single function f_θ(T_B) → {π_k, μ_{k,c}, s_{k,c}}_{k=1..K, c=1..3} — i.e., 7K output channels per pixel (1 weight + 3 means + 3 log-scales per component). This single head **is** Stage 2 and Stage 3 of every prior architectural revision, merged: π_k(T_B) plays exactly the role the earlier "radiometric class latent" played, except it is no longer a separate trained sub-system — it falls directly out of the mixture's own weight vector, learned purely to maximize likelihood. There is no longer a justification for a separate classification module of any kind, supervised or self-supervised; it was always implicit in the mixture weights.

**4.4 — Training objective (loss).**

L(θ) = − (1/N) Σ_{i=1}^{N} log [ Σ_{k=1}^{K} π_k(T_{B,i}) · Π_c DiscLogistic(x_{i,c}; μ_{k,c}, s_{k,c}) ]

computed with the standard log-sum-exp stabilization. This is the entire training objective for the colorization sub-problem — one loss, no auxiliary terms, no λ-weighted side objectives. (The SR sub-problem retains its own separate, deterministic loss, justified independently in §5 below — combining the two into one objective is not proposed and would not be appropriate, since they are different statistical problems.)

**4.5 — Inference.** Forward pass yields {π_k, μ_k, s_k} in closed form — no sampling, no iteration. Two downstream quantities are computed from these parameters directly:

- **Point estimate (reported color):** μ_{k*}, where k* = argmax_k π_k — the mean of the **dominant component**, not the mixture mean Σ_k π_k μ_k. This distinction is the direct mathematical fix for the failure mode proven in §1/§2: the mixture mean reintroduces the invalid-blend problem at one remove; the dominant-component mean does not, because it always corresponds to one internally coherent physical hypothesis.

- **Confidence / uncertainty.** By the law of total variance,
Var[RGB | T_B] = Σ_k π_k · s_k² · c_logistic + Σ_k π_k (μ_k − μ̄)², μ̄ = Σ_k π_k μ_k
The first term is **within-mode** uncertainty (genuine radiometric/measurement noise around one physical hypothesis). The second term is **between-mode** uncertainty (disagreement among distinct, competing physical hypotheses — i.e., genuine material ambiguity). Reporting these as two separate confidence channels, rather than one collapsed number, is scientifically more informative and costs nothing extra: both terms are already present in the closed-form output of a single forward pass. Separately, the entropy of {π_k} is a direct, interpretable multimodality signal usable as an independent confidence cue ("this pixel is ambiguous between materials") distinct from per-mode scale ("this pixel's dominant hypothesis is itself noisy").

## 5. Evaluation against the official BAH criteria (strictly, no scope expansion)

- **Super resolution.** Unaffected in formulation, and the formulation clarifies *why* it is unaffected: the TIR SR problem, conditioned on a known degradation operator, is far closer to a well-posed (near-unique) inverse problem than colorization, so deterministic regression with a physics-consistency loss remains the mathematically appropriate tool there. The probabilistic reformulation does not need to — and should not — be applied to Stage 1; stating this explicitly strengthens the proposal's internal coherence, since it shows the choice between deterministic and probabilistic treatment is principled per sub-problem, not applied uniformly out of habit.

- **RGB colorization.** Directly and entirely the subject of §4 — the colorization output is no longer a guess but the closed-form summary (dominant-mode mean) of an explicitly estimated conditional distribution.

- **Structural integrity.** Reporting the dominant-component mean rather than the mixture mean is itself a structural-integrity mechanism: at a material boundary pixel, the mixture-mean estimator would blend two materials' colors into a third, spatially smeared value that exists at no real boundary; the dominant-mode estimator keeps each pixel anchored to one coherent material hypothesis, preserving sharp, physically meaningful transitions rather than synthesizing a soft, anatomically incorrect gradient between classes.

- **Hallucination reduction.** This is no longer a design philosophy but a measurable property of the loss function itself: confident wrong answers are exactly what occurs when a single-mode estimator is forced onto a multimodal target (§1, §2); the mixture formulation is the direct mathematical countermeasure, and its success is verifiable via the between-mode variance term and the sparsification evaluation already proposed.

- **Physics-informed modelling.** P enters as the deterministic calibration transform (§4.1); the within-mode/between-mode variance decomposition (§4.5) is itself a physical statement — between-mode variance is, by construction, attributable to the documented physical fact that thermal emission and visible reflectance are governed by different surface properties (§1.2 of the original proposal), giving every term in the loss a stated physical referent rather than an abstract probabilistic add-on.

- **Low inference time per tile.** A single forward pass produces {π_k, μ_k, s_k} in closed form; the point estimate and both uncertainty terms are algebraic functions of these outputs, requiring no sampling, no MCMC, and no multi-step integration. This is the central, decisive argument for rejecting every heavier alternative in §3.

## 6. Implementation derived directly from the mathematics

Ask only: what is the minimum machinery required to produce {π_k, μ_{k,c}, s_{k,c}} from T_B, plus the independently-justified SR output?

**One shared backbone → two heads.**
1. **SR head** (deterministic regression on calibrated T_B; loss and justification unchanged from prior reviews — this sub-problem remains deterministic because it is comparatively well-posed, §5).
2. **Mixture head** (7K output channels operating on the super-resolved 100 m features, since both deliverables are required at 100 m; loss exactly as derived in §4.4).

No separate classification stage. No codebook. No vector-quantization layer. No calibration-loss term. No perceptual loss term. Every one of those, in every prior architectural revision, existed only because the algorithm had not yet been stated in closed form as a single conditional density. Once stated, they collapse into a single output head and a single likelihood. This is the leanest version of VARNA that has appeared across this entire review process, and it is leaner *because* it is more correct, not despite it.

## 7. Failure analysis

- **Scientific assumption — fixed, small K.** A mixture with K components can only represent up to K distinct physical hypotheses per local context; if a tile genuinely contains more than K materials sharing similar T_B, modes merge. *Mitigation:* sweep K against held-out NLL (a hyperparameter search over an output-width integer, not an architecture change) and pick the smallest K past which NLL stops improving — cheap, standard, and bounded in scope.

- **Dataset assumption — training-region coverage.** The mixture is only as good as the joint (T_B, RGB) statistics observed in training regions; under geographic or seasonal distribution shift, both π_k and the mode locations will be miscalibrated. *Mitigation:* report predictive variance honestly as a proxy for this risk rather than attempting to correct for it within the hackathon timeline — an explicit, stated limitation rather than an unvalidated fix.

- **Physical assumption — calibration residual noise.** P assumes accurate sensor constants and adequate atmospheric correction in the Level-2 product; residual noise inflates within-mode scale s_k, conflating measurement noise with genuine material ambiguity in the within-mode variance term. *Mitigation:* state this conflation explicitly as a documented limitation; disentangling it is future work, not a hackathon deliverable.

- **Deployment assumption — numerical stability under quantization.** The log-sum-exp and sigmoid-difference computations in the discretized-logistic likelihood are sensitive to low precision. *Mitigation:* keep the final mixture-likelihood arithmetic in FP32 even if the shared backbone runs FP16 — a standard, low-cost mixed-precision practice, not a redesign.

- **Corner case — near-homogeneous tiles.** Open water or uniform desert should collapse the mixture to one dominant, tight component. *Mitigation:* verify this emerges naturally from NLL minimization alone before adding anything; if it does not, a small entropy regularizer discouraging unnecessary use of multiple components is the minimal, scientifically justified fallback — not a new module, a one-line addition to the existing loss.

## 9. Final reduction

**One sentence.** VARNA reframes thermal-to-visible colorization from learning a function RGB = f(T_B) to estimating a conditional distribution p(RGB | T_B) as a discretized logistic mixture, because the true relationship between thermal radiance and visible reflectance is provably one-to-many, not one-to-one.

**One equation.**
p(RGB | T_B) = Σ_{k=1}^{K} π_k(T_B) · Π_{c} [σ((x_c+0.5−μ_{k,c})/s_{k,c}) − σ((x_c−0.5−μ_{k,c})/s_{k,c})], trained by maximizing this likelihood directly.

**One figure.** Shown above — a single thermal pixel conditions a distribution with two real, physically grounded modes; a deterministic regressor would instead target the invalid midpoint between them, which the figure marks explicitly as the failure case this formulation is designed to avoid.

**One paragraph.** Thermal radiance and visible-light reflectance are governed by different physical processes — emission versus reflection — so a single thermal measurement is consistent with more than one physically real surface color. Every prior image-translation approach (GAN-based or diffusion-based) either ignores this fact, producing a single confident but potentially invalid color, or represents it only implicitly through expensive repeated sampling. VARNA instead predicts the small set of physically plausible colors directly, in one forward pass, reports the most likely one, and — critically — reports *how confident it is and why*, by separating uncertainty into "this pixel is genuinely ambiguous between materials" and "this pixel's most likely material is itself noisy." This is the entire scientific contribution; every remaining engineering choice exists only to compute it efficiently within ISRO's operational latency budget.

The formulation survives this review without modification beyond the single discretized-logistic correction in §3. No heavier representation is justified for a three-channel, low-latency, four-engineer hackathon deliverable, and no lighter representation (deterministic regression) is mathematically defensible given the physics established in the original problem analysis.
